<p class="intro"><span class="dropcap">I've</span> spent a lot of time recently reflecting on the reality behind the hype of AI-driven coding tools and agentic workflows. Initially, I embraced these tools—Plural Code, Claude Code, and others—with genuine excitement, seeing in them the promise of faster product development, fewer tedious tasks, and the ability to build things I’d previously thought impossible or too time-consuming.</p>

However, over the past few months, a sobering reality has set in. While these tools are undoubtedly impressive and groundbreaking in their capabilities, they come with a hidden cost: my own time, energy, and attention. Instead of spending less time coding, I’m now spending more time orchestrating an AI agent—babysitting it, correcting its mistakes, carefully curating its context, and ensuring it remains productive and on-task.

<p class="quote">“We traded craft for coordination. We traded code for control. We traded weekends for workflows.”</p>

## The Fundamental Problem: Immutable Minds

At its core, today’s Large Language Models (LLMs) have a fundamental limitation: they’re static. Despite being powerful, they don’t truly learn—not in the way a human engineer does. They don’t build intuition, accumulate genuine memories, or organically become experts in my specific projects and workflows. They simply predict the next token based on carefully provided context.

The tools built around these LLMs vary primarily in how well they manage this context—avoiding “context pollution” by finding exactly the right snippets of code and tasks to provide. Tools like Claude Code do this better than others, but the reality remains the same: they’re managing constraints, not creating true autonomy.

The Context Window Conundrum

This constraint isn’t minor—it’s fundamental. The context window (the “memory” of an LLM) is finite and expensive, especially at scale. Sure, strategies exist to mitigate these constraints, but they all rely on clever engineering to patch an underlying weakness.

In software engineering, where the complexity and scale of projects continuously evolve, these limitations become painfully apparent. Human engineers, with limitless context windows and genuine memory and intuition, still outperform any current LLM-based tool in tasks that require deep understanding and learning over time.

<p class="quote">“Today’s AI tools aren’t learning; they’re reconstructing. You’re orchestrating autocomplete, not collaborating with a true partner.”</p>

## The Hidden Cost: AI-Induced Burnout

Initially, I viewed these tools as a pathway to faster productivity and better use of my time—more focus on building products and less time on the minutiae of coding. Ironically, the opposite has happened. The promise of AI has become a form of pressure: I find myself feeling guilty if I’m not constantly making use of the expensive subscription I’ve bought into, even losing sleep over whether I’ve optimised its usage enough.

Worse, this “optimisation” often involves endless handholding, fine-tuning workflows, and correcting assumptions made by my AI assistant. The mental load has simply shifted from traditional coding to managing AI workflows.

<p class="quote">“The magic fades when you realise you’re babysitting a glorified intern who forgets everything each time they blink.”</p>

## Striking the Right Balance

My realisation is that this isn’t sustainable—not practically and certainly not emotionally. The reason I wanted to use these AI tools in the first place was to reclaim time and mental space to focus on what truly matters to me: my family, my life, and the joy of creating products that people love.

Ultimately, AI tools can and do offer extraordinary power. But if we’re not careful, they can also exact an extraordinary toll. Perhaps the greatest breakthrough isn’t technological—it’s personal. It’s realising that the real magic still resides within us, the creators and thinkers who imagined these tools in the first place.

<p class="quote">“AI should augment us, not absorb us. The real breakthrough is remembering why we create—and reclaiming the joy of building.”</p>

I’m stepping back a bit, recalibrating how I engage with these tools. I refuse to let the pressure of AI productivity rob me of the human joys of creating, building, and spending time with those I love. And perhaps, ironically, that might just be the most productive decision I’ve ever made.
